About {{name}}

Project Overview

The {{name}} is a tool designed for linguistic analysis and data preparation. It serves as a playground for understanding how text normalization impacts data metrics.

Mathematical Foundations

The application utilizes Zipf's Law to visualize word frequency distribution. Zipf's law states that given some corpus of natural language utterances, the frequency of any word is inversely proportional to its rank in the frequency table.

Mathematically, this can be expressed as: \[ f(k; s, N) = \frac{1/k^s}{\sum_{n=1}^{N} (1/n^s)} \] Where:

  • \( k \) is the rank of the word.
  • \( s \) is the value of the exponent characterizing the distribution.
  • \( N \) is the number of elements.

Pipeline Logic

The core processing pipeline follows a linear transformation model: \[ T_{final} = S_n(S_{n-1}(...S_1(T_{raw})...)) \] Each step \( S_i \) is a discrete transformation applied to the text tokens, allowing for modular and testable code.

How it works

  1. Tokenization: Raw text is broken down into individual words (tokens) using NLTK's word_tokenize.
  2. Cleaning: Depending on user selection, punctuation is removed, and non-alphabetic characters are filtered.
  3. Normalization: Text is optionally converted to lowercase to ensure uniform counting.
  4. Stop Word Removal: Common "filler" words (e.g., "the", "is", "at") can be removed to focus on meaningful content.
  5. Metrics Generation: The final processed list of words is used to calculate counts and frequency distributions.
